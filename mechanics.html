CHARACTER STATS
We have no plans to employ levels or advancement systems outside of occasional plot-related bonuses or earnable rewards. Your character will only have the following mechanical aspects to keep track of: stats, hit points, skills, and foci. This might seem like a lot, but it's actually all quite simple!
The rundown is this:
  • Stats represent how physically capable your character is.
  • Hit points represent how close your character is to defeat.
  • Skills represent your character's talents and prowess.
  • Foci represent additional abilities and skills your character has.
STATS

Stats are divided into Strength, Fortitude, and Dexterity. These are strictly the physical boundaries of your character; we've decided not to include things like charisma and intelligence as hard numbers, because these things fall under the jurisdiction of your skills.

Stats can go up to 18. Players have a total of 30 points they can allocate, with each stat having a minimum of 3. 10 and 11 are considered what's average.

When rolling dice that are affected by a stat, such as a roll made to push a boulder or win a footrace, you don't apply the whole stat; instead, you apply a modifier. This modifier is a small change applied to a relevant roll as a nod to your character's strength or weakness in that area.

  • score of 3: -2
  • score of 4-7: -1
  • score of 8-13: 0
  • score of 14-17: +1
  • score of 18: +2
HIT POINTS

Hit points are the quantification of how close your character is to being defeated.

A successful attack doesn’t always mean a direct physical hit. An attack that forces an enemy off-balance, grazes him, frightens him, or exhausts him might all drain his hit points, bringing him closer to defeat without actually inflicting real physical harm. Only the last handful of hit points necessarily reflect serious bodily damage, so it’s up to the GM and player to decide how to describe the effects of a successful hit.

Hit points err on the lower side to avoid bloat and make combat short. Your total hit points will be 10 + your Fortitude modifier, plus anything granted by your focus.

SKILLS

Skills are all the things your character can do that rely on honed proficiency, be it your intelligence, your affability, or your general handiness and quick-thinking. Unlike popular game systems such as Dungeons & Dragons, skills here aren't connected to any one stat, and don't make use of modifiers the way our three physical stats do.

Instead, skills are done in a tier system, with characters being able to have up to tier 5 in any given skill. These tiers represent your level of understanding and proficiency with a certain topic, be it engineering or medicine or even just how to impress and flatter people.

These are what the different skill tiers mean:

  • untrained: You have no experience with this skill, and have no idea what you're doing.
  • tier 1: You have very basic, ground-level knowledge about this skill.
  • tier 2: You have experience with this skill, and can do most basic tasks.
  • tier 3: Your mastery of this skill allows you to complete difficult challenges.
  • tier 4: You're an expert in this skill, and very few things escape your capabilities.
  • tier 5: You're a complete expert in this skill, probably even the best on the ship. Your small amount of failures are due to circumstance, not a lack of competency.
Skills checks, rolls done to overcome obstacles, represent challenges to a character's capabilities - for example, a Pilot check might be made to navigate an asteroid field safely, and a Heal check might be made to treat someone's wounds efficiently. These checks all have a difficulty level, also represented in tiers. You will always succeed at tasks with a difficulty level under your current tier in a skill. This represents your training and expertise in that field. After all, a master engineer wouldn't fumble with screwing plates together, nor would someone with leadership experience fail to motivate already-eager troops!

When attempting a task with a difficulty at your current tier level, you roll 1d20. So, for example, if a character with 2 tiers in the Heal skill were caring for a group of wounded soldiers, they'd be able to apply bandages and give others their prognosis without bothering to roll, but would still need to complete a skill check to do something such as amputating a limb or performing thoracic surgery. In this case, you need to roll a 13 or higher in order for your skill check to be successful.

A task a tier above your own needs a 15 or higher to be successful. If attempting a task with a difficulty two tiers above your own, you roll with disadvantage - 2d20, with the lower roll being the one that counts. Attempts at tiers beyond that are counted as automatic failures.

Some items or situations, such as manuals or another character helping you, may grant you something called an asset in a certain skill, which is a temporary bonus to your capabilities. Assets allow you to roll for a skill check one tier above your own with advantage - 2d20, with the higher roll being the one that counts! Some assets may only grant this bonus up to a certain point, such as a pilot's manual that might only be useful to characters with tier 2 or lower in Pilot, but contains no new information for anyone with tier 3 and above.

When just starting out, you may only have up to tier 2 in any skill, with exceptions for high ranking officers (jobs with a star next to their title on the manifest), who can have up to tier 3 in one skill only. New characters get three free tiers to allocate, in addition to those provided by their job and focus.

There are, of course, difficulty tiers for skill checks beyond tier 5, which represent attempts to understand things your civilization hasn't researched or encountered before, or other such situations a character couldn't realistically be prepared for.

INTERPERSONAL SKILLS
ADMINISTER. Manage an organization, handle paperwork, analyze records, and keep an institution functioning on a daily basis. Roll it for bureaucratic expertise, organizational management, legal knowledge, dealing with government agencies, and understanding how institutions really work.
CONNECT. Find people who can be helpful to your purposes and get them to cooperate with you. Roll it to make useful connections with others, find people you know, know where to get illicit goods and services, and be familiar with foreign cultures and languages. You can use it in place of talk for persuading people you find via this skill.
LEAD. Convince others to also do whatever it is you're trying to do. Talk might persuade them that following you is smart, but lead can make them do it even when they think it's a bad idea. Roll it to lead troops in combat, convince others to follow you, or maintain morale and discipline.
TALK. Convince other people of the facts you want them to believe. Roll it to persuade, charm, or deceive others in conversation.
TRADE. Find what you need on the market and sell what you have. Roll it to sell things, figure out where to purchase hard-to-get goods, or haggle with merchants.
OCCUPATIONAL SKILLS
FIX. Create and repair devices both simple and complex. Roll it to fix things, build things, and identify what something is supposed to do.
HEAL. Employ medical and psychological treatment for the injured. Roll it to cure diseases, stabilize the critically injured, treat psychological disorders, or diagnose illnesses.
KNOW. Know facts about academic or scientific fields. roll it to understand planetary ecologies, remember relevant history, solve science mysteries, and know the basic facts about rare or esoteric topics.
PERFORM. Exhibit some performative skill. roll it to dance, sing, orate, act, or otherwise put on a convincing or emotionally moving performance.
PILOT. Use this skill to pilot vehicles or ride beasts. roll it to fly spaceships, drive vehicles, ride animals, or tend to basic vehicle repair.
PROGRAM. Operating or hacking computing and communications hardware. Roll it to program or hack computers, control computer-operated hardware, operate communications tech, or decrypt things.
WORK. This is a catch-all skill for professions not represented by other skills. Roll it to work at a particular profession, art, or trade.
COMBAT SKILLS
PUNCH. Use it as a combat skill when fighting unarmed.
SHOOT. Use it as a combat skill when using ranged weaponry, whether hurled rocks, bows, laser pistols, combat rifles, or ship's gunnery.
STAB. Use it as a combat skill when wielding melee weapons, whether primitive or complex.
ENVIRONMENTAL SKILLS
NOTICE. Spot anomalies or interesting facts about your environment. Roll it for searching places, detecting ambushes, catching lies, spotting things, and reading the emotional state of other people.
SNEAK. Move without drawing notice. Roll it for stealth, disguise, infiltration, pickpocketing, and the defeat of security measures.
SURVIVE. Obtain the basics of food, water, and shelter in hostile environments, along with avoiding their natural perils. Roll it to handle animals, navigate difficult terrain, scrounge for resources, make basic tools, and avoid wild beasts or people hunting you.
FOCI

Foci represent your character's unique specialization or talent, such as in hobbies, former lines of work, or a niche in a broader field of study. Anyone is welcome to write their own focus for their character, so long as they're appropriately balanced in comparison to what's provided. A single focus will confer up to three benefits, be it a situational advantage, a couple more hit points, one tier in any skill, or some other sort of nifty bonus.

WORK IN PROGRESS.

TWO MEN ENTER, ONE MAN LEAVES
It's usually best to find a means of success that doesn't involve brute-forcing your way through an encounter - however, sometimes blasting a bunch of suckers in the face with your laser pistol is simply unavoidable. This is where combat comes in! Combat is just a way of seeing which side has prevailed over the other in battle, and through what means. There are two ways to do combat: long and short.

Typical combat is done over a google sheets playing field called the THUNDERDOME, with each square representing three feet. You will be able to move your character's tokens via edit permissions on any relevant sheet, as well as write in your actions on the turn order.

In addition to any relevant dice rolls, combat sessions may be actively discussed in the #thunderdome channel of the discord. This is also where the peanut gallery comments go - feel free to be as much of a loud, zany hypeman as your heart desires.

If you've played a tabletop game before, it's likely you already know how most of this stuff works. I know there's much texto here, so feel free to just skim!

ROUNDS

Combat is divided up into rounds, and rounds are divided up into turns. There is no hard limit as to how many rounds can be in one session of combat. Each round is approximately ten seconds, maybe even shorter, during which all participants act. When everyone has had their turn, the round ends, and another one begins from the top.

INITIATIVE

In 1v1 combat, whoever has the highest Dexterity stat goes first. If their Dexterity stats are the same, each character rolls a 1d20 and adds their Dexterity modifier. In combat with more than two opposed participants, however, things get slightly more complicated.

In any group combat scenario, there are going to be sides. Sides are comprised of any participants who are allied to each other, and are fighting together for the same goal. The guy trying to shoot you is not on your side, but your coworker and your best friend are. During group combat, players will be asked to put their character on a side before the encounter starts. Technically, the option to defect and assist the enemy is always there, but this opens you up to attack and consequence from your fellow crewmen.

Initiative in group combat works like this: the individuals on each side with the highest Dexterity stat roll a 1d20, and add their Dexterity modifier. The character who rolled the highest has their side go first - initiative within any given side is determined the exact same way it is in 1v1 combat, by descending Dexterity.

Occasionally, however, combat may be sprung unexpectedly upon an individual or upon a group. In that case, the character or side performing an ambush is automatically first in the turn order, despite their Dexterity stat, as their victim is surprised. Player characters aren't subject to surprise unless their guard is genuinely down at the moment of the ambush. Someone walking in a dangerous part of a city is unlikely to be shocked by the sudden appearance of danger, unless the attack is so sudden and from such an unexpected quarter that even their obvious caution is insufficient.
ACTION ECONOMY

There are three different types of actions a character can perform: the Main Action, the Move Action, and the Instant Action.

MAIN ACTION | one on your turn
Every character can make one Main Action on their turn. Main Actions are deeds that absorb most of their turn, such as attacking an enemy, bracing a door, applying first aid to a downed ally, manipulating a complex device, or some other activity that takes up their focus. In place of their Main Action, a character can choose to take a second Move action.
  • making a melee attack. An opponent is within melee range of you if they're on a tile adjacent to yours, including diagonally. A weapon must be readied to be attacked with.
  • making a ranged attack. Ranged weapons often have a maximum range, so be sure to check what the range on your weapon is! Ranged attacks cannot be made if an opponent with a melee weapon is on a tile adjacent to yours.
  • disengaging. A character who wants to disengage from an opponent in melee must use their Main Action to do so, or else they suffer an attack of opportunity from any foe engaged with them when they perform their Move Action. There is no need to spend a Main Action disengaging from enemies who are wielding ranged weapons.
  • using a skill. Any skill that can plausibly be applied in ten seconds may be used as a main action. This could be brief medical care, an inspiring rallying cry, tinkering with a broken weapon to get it operational, or anything else you can come up with.
MOVE ACTION | one on your turn
Every character can make one Move Action on their turn, moving a number of tiles on the map equal to their Dexterity stat. Movement can be done diagonally. Characters can't split their Move Action, such as taking part of it before using their Main Action and the rest afterwards - all movement must be done in one swift stroke. Movement from the Move Action can be sacrificed to use this segment of your turn in some other ways, detailed below.
  • picking up an item. A character can scoop up an object off the ground if they have a free hand, leaving said object readied in their grip.
  • reloading. The character swaps magazines, or, god forbid, loads an arrow into some sort of space crossbow.
  • grabbing or stowing something. Only one of the two can be done per move action. The character shoves something in their backpack or pockets, or draws something instead. Drawn objects are automatically readied for use.
  • going prone or standing up. Only one of the two can be done per move action. Going prone makes a character harder to hit by ranged attackers, but makes them easier to hit for anyone adjacent to them. Characters must use their Move Action to stand up from the prone state.
  • holding an action. By choosing to Hold An Action, the combatant can delay taking the rest of their actions until later in the round, activating them as an Instant action. Thus, a character at the top of the turn order might use their Move action to Hold An Action. Later in the round, as an enemy rushes around a corner, they may instantly elect to use their Main Action to make a ranged attack with their readied laser rifle.
  • shifting grip on a ranged weapon. Certain ranged weapons can be shifted in one's hands to be used as a crude melee weapon, such as using the butt of a rifle or the back of a pistol. If applicable to your weapon, stats for use in melee will be available. This action can also be taken to wield a shifted ranged weapon for shooting once again.
INSTANT ACTION | any amount, any time
An Instant action is special in that it can be performed even when it's not the actor's turn. Certain combat actions are Instant, though more impactful ones often cost the actor's Main Action for the round as well. Dropping an object, saying something brief, or activating a simple device are all examples of Instant Actions. Any number of Instant Actions can be performed during a round, so long as any requirements are able to be met.
  • snap attacks. A combatant with a weapon readied can rush their attack, sacrificing accuracy for speed. The combatant must still have their Main Action impending or available. As an Instant Action, they can sacrifice it to make a melee or ranged attack against a foe in range. This attack takes a -4 penalty to its hit roll.
  • total defense. As an Instant action, the combatant focuses on nothing other than avoiding knives, gunfire, hurled crystalline spears, or other such perils. Using Total Defense costs the user their Main Action for the round, and can't be used if they've already employed their Main Action. They become immune to Shock damage for the round, regardless of their Armor Class, and gain a +2 bonus to their Armor Class until their next turn.
HITTING AN ENEMY

When a character wants to hit a target, they need to use their Main Action to make an attack roll. An attack roll represents not just one lucky shot or swing, but them spending their round trying to get in a good hit or a clean shot at their target.

To make an attack roll, the assailant rolls 1d20 and adds the number of tiers they have in their applicable skill (Punch, Shoot, or Stab), and the stat modifier most relevant to the weapon (ranged weapons use Dexterity, small melee weapons can use Strength or Dexterity, and large melee weapons use Strength). If the total is equal to or higher than the target's Armor Class, the attack hits.

A successful attack inflicts damage to the target's hit points equal to the weapon's damage die. If it's an unarmed attack using the Punch skill, the attacker gets to add their tier number to the damage as well. If the target's Armor Class is low enough, some melee weapons will apply Shock, which is a minimum amount of damage taken by the target even if their Armor Class isn't beaten on the attack roll.

Certain circumstances in combat can make it easier or harder to land a blow on an opponent. For example, a marksman might have a hard time getting a bead on someone laying prone, but a melee attacker with a club will have a pretty easy time beating them senseless. Below are a table of modifiers to attack rolls based on positioning on the battlefield.
  • Shooting at a distant prone foe: -2
  • Attacking an adjacent prone foe: +2
  • Melee attacking while prone: -4
  • The target is at least half behind cover: -2
  • The target is almost completely in cover: -4
  • Shooting from a prepared firing position: +2
EDGE CASES

dual wielding. Dual-wielding requires at least tier 1 in Shoot or Stab depending on the weapons used. Anyone dual-wielding suffers a -1 penalty on hit rolls due to the extra complication, but gains a +2 bonus on damage rolls on a successful hit. Dual-wielding does not apply to fists.

shoving or disarming an enemy. To shove, herd, or otherwise manipulate a foe, the attacker has to hit them with a Punch attack, doing no damage, and then win an opposed Punch skill check. If they win, the opponent either gets moved up to three meters in the direction the player wishes, is disarmed, or is knocked prone. Disarmed weapons are dropped on the ground, and a Move Action must be used to pick them up.
SHORT COMBAT

If you don't feel like running a full combat session for a 1v1 fistfight, then, good news! Neither do I!

When long combat in any given scenario wouldn't help pacing or be interesting for the player characters, short combat is what we use. Short combat is excellent for scenes where the how and what of combat doesn't matter, only the resolution does.

Short combat doesn't have initiative, or rounds, or anything like that. Instead, opposed sides consider their defenses and offenses: that is, each side lists off what makes their defense fortified, and their offense strong.

This can be as arbitrary as you want, from 'my space sword is huge and I sharpened it this morning, making me able to cut through armor effectively', to 'my enemy broke his leg last week and I didn't', to 'I am feeling extremely replenished by the giant breakfast I had, and have a lot of energy to spare in combat'. The only restriction is that these things must be reasonably applicable to whichever category you put it in: offense or defense. Get creative!

Beginning when a link to the relevant googledoc is posted, each side has three minutes to cobble together their offensive and defensive boons. The linked document will have a template for categories ready to go by the time the clock starts, so combatants only have to worry about how fast they can come up with and jot down their boons. When three minutes are up, it's time to roll.

Each side tallies the number of things written down for both offense and defense, and then rolls: a number of d4 equal to how many items are in the offense category, and another number of d4 equal to how many are in the defense category. Each side then compares their offense roll to the other's defense roll. If the offense roll doesn't exceed the other side's defense roll, no damage has been dealt; they successfully held their own. If it does exceed the defense roll, then what matters is by how much. The side whose offense roll exceeded the opposing defense the most is considered the victor of that session of combat.

Short combat doesn't always need to be done for the express purpose of killing; it can be done to get guards out of the way, disarm a group of enemies, or even render them unconscious for the purpose of cartoonishly tying them up. The victors of short combat get to describe the outcome of the battle.

Player characters can't die in short combat, but may be knocked unconscious or otherwise harmed and beaten. Winners of short combat may not choose to inflict particularly harsh punishments upon other player characters - this privilege is exclusively for NPCs. If a group of player characters end up losing short combat, all have their current Hit Points halved.
EXAMPLE

SIDE 1 has managed to tally 10 items for their offense and 5 for their defense. SIDE 2 has 6 items for their offense and 10 for their defense. SIDE 1 rolls 10d4 and 5d4. They get a total of 25 and 10 each. SIDE 2 rolls 6d4 and 10d4. They get 14 and 25 each.

SIDE 1 does not manage to penetrate SIDE 2's defenses, as their offensive roll of 25 does not exceed the defensive roll of 25. However, SIDE 2's offensive roll of 14 exceeds SIDE 1's defensive roll by 4. SIDE 2 wins the combat scene, and describes what has happened to SIDE 1.
LOSING HIT POINTS

When a character runs out of hit points, they become wounded. A wounded character is unconscious or otherwise unable to act for the rest of the combat encounter, but can continue on as usual afterwards, albeit suffering the ramifications of their wounds. A character's wounded status is designated by a crutch symbol on their profile on the roster.

Wounds can be shed in the following ways:
  • submission of an art or writing piece depicting gaining or treating the wound,
  • two weeks of no combat,
  • a short roleplay with someone in the medical department, or
  • purchase of a quick fix at the replicator.
When a character's entire pool of hit points is reduced from full to zero in a single blow, they've sustained especially grievous damage. In this situation, a character may choose to either succumb to death or take a permanent injury. Characters who receive injuries also receive a wound, and must deal with the wound as normal - injuries are only the persistent effects of said wound.
Injuries are rolled randomly from the following table unless otherwise specified:
MAJOR SCAR. Either from surgery or from the attack itself, you now have a disfiguring scar somewhere on your body. If it's outside an area usually covered by clothes, it's most likely extremely noticeable. This scar could be a deep rake across your face, a long angry cut down your torso, or the remnants of a hasty surgery or a messy bulletwound - anything, so long as it's obviously more than just an average scratch or scrape. The specifics of the scar are up to the player. Major scars are a double-edged sword. While your scar is visible, you have advantage on Lead checks made to command or inspire allies, but suffer disadvantage on Talk checks made to charm civilians.
MINOR SCAR. Either from surgery or from the attack itself, you now have a scar somewhere on your body. Thankfully, it's not too noticeable. You don't suffer any penalties for this injury aside from a small scar, the specifics of which are up to the player.
LOST EYE. Your eye was either completely lost in battle, or enucleated afterwards due to being unsalvageable. The good news: you can now wear eyepatches and not feel silly doing it! Missing an eye comes with the complication of reduced depth perception. You have disadvantage on Pilot and Shoot checks. If you lose all your eyes, you become blind. While blind, you are unable to make Notice, Pilot, or Program checks that rely on sight, and suffer double disadvantage on Shoot, Stab, and Punch checks (4d20, take lowest).
LOST ARM. Your arm was either blown off your body during the fight, or mangled so badly that it had to be amputated. For the purpose of alien biology, arms are defined as anything used to manipulate objects. Upon losing an arm, you gain disadvantage on Fix, Heal, Perform, and Work checks which require use of two arms, as well as Shoot and Stab checks using two-handed weapons. If you lose all your arms, you become unable to perform Fix, Heal, Perform, Program, Work, Shoot, and Stab checks which require use of the arms.
LOST LEG. Your leg was either blown off your body during the fight, or mangled so badly that it had to be amputated. For the purpose of alien biology, legs are defined as anything used to perform locomotion. Upon losing a leg, you gain disadvantage on Dexterity rolls, as well as Sneak checks. If you lose all your legs, you automatically fail all Dexterity rolls versus a difficulty class of 8 or more, and require assistance to move.
HEAD INJURY. You've sustained a blow to the head, rendering your thoughts fuzzy and unorganized. Upon gaining a head injury, your skills are treated as if they were one tier lower.
LIMP. Your leg was damaged in combat, but has mostly been fixed, save for a sharp pain whenever too much weight is put on it. Because of this, you've been forced into an uncomfortable, awkward gait that's significantly slower than average. Upon gaining a limp, characters suffer disadvantage on Dexterity rolls. A character unfortunate enough to roll a limp on two separate occasions will end up losing a leg.

These injuries all have means of treatment available at the replicator - both types of scars can be removed by different strengths of dermal regeneration formulas, head injuries can be treated with neuroregeneratives, and lost body parts can be replaced. Limps can be counteracted in the form of orthoses, but require corrective surgery to completely eliminate.

If a character who's already wounded is reduced to zero hit points, they die. Additionally, any character that takes twice their hit points worth of damage in a single attack dies outright.
